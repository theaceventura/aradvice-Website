Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between cyber security and cyber governance?

Cyber security involves the technical controls like firewalls and encryption, while cyber governance for boards australia is the strategic framework for digital risk accountability. Security is about how we protect data; governance is about who is responsible and why certain risks are tolerated. It ensures that technical activities align with the board's fiduciary duties and the organisation's overall risk appetite.

Can a board be held legally liable for a cyber breach in Australia?

Directors can be held personally liable for governance failures following a breach under Section 180 of the Corporations Act. With the Privacy Act 1988 now carrying penalties up to $50 million, the legal standard for reasonable steps has increased. Regulators like ASIC now scrutinise whether a board exercised due diligence in overseeing digital resilience before an incident occurred.

How often should the board review the organisation’s cyber posture?

Boards should review their cyber posture continuously through monthly reporting, with a strategic deep-dive conducted at least every quarter. The 2023–2030 Australian Cyber Security Strategy emphasises that resilience is a moving target. High-impact reviews should occur more frequently when adopting emerging technologies or when the threat landscape shifts, such as the 280% increase in DDoS attacks recorded in 2025.

What are the top three questions every director should ask their CISO?

First, ask how management claims are independently verified to ensure an unfiltered view of risk. Second, enquire about the specific business impact of the top three digital vulnerabilities. Third, ask who is accountable for the security of our third-party supply chains. These questions pivot the conversation from technical metrics to the core principles of defensible oversight and legal accountability.

Do we need a cyber expert on the board to ensure effective oversight?

You don't need a technical expert on the board, but every director requires enough digital literacy to provide effective cyber governance for boards australia. The AICD and ACS both suggest that the board's collective responsibility is to challenge management's assumptions. Relying on a single expert can create a dangerous silo; instead, the entire board must understand risk in the language of governance.

How does AI governance differ from traditional cyber governance?

AI governance focuses on the transparency and ethics of automated decision-making, whereas traditional cyber governance prioritises data confidentiality and system integrity. New transparency obligations coming on 10 December 2026 require organisations to disclose how personal information is used in AI models. This adds a layer of ethical accountability that goes beyond standard technical security controls and requires direct board oversight.

Why is an independent review better than an internal audit for cyber?

An independent review provides a conflict-free assessment that an internal audit or a technical vendor cannot offer. Internal teams often have a vested interest in justifying their own performance or budget. An independent advisor has no implementation ties, ensuring that the board receives a sober, unbiased evaluation of whether their oversight actually meets 2026 regulatory standards and fiduciary requirements.

What happens during a board-level incident simulation?

A board-level simulation is a facilitated exercise that stress-tests your decision-making under the pressure of a real breach. It forces you to navigate the 12-hour reporting windows of the SOCI Act and the 72-hour mandates of the Cyber Security Act 2024. These workshops move past technical recovery to focus on strategic communication, legal obligations, and the preservation of shareholder trust during a crisis.
