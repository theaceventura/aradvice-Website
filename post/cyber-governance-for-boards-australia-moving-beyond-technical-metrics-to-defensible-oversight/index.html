Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between cyber security and cyber governance?

Cyber security focuses on the technical implementation of controls to protect systems, while cyber governance for boards australia involves the strategic oversight and accountability of those systems. Security is what your IT team does to protect the perimeter; governance is how the board ensures those actions align with the organisation's risk appetite. It's about shifting from technical metrics to a defensible oversight framework that satisfies regulatory scrutiny and protects shareholder value.

Can a board be held legally liable for a cyber breach in Australia?

Australian directors can be held personally liable for cyber breaches under Section 180 of the Corporations Act 2001. Failure to manage cyber risk is now viewed by ASIC as a failure of care and diligence. In 2022, the Federal Court's ruling against RI Advice confirmed that inadequate cybersecurity is a breach of licence obligations. This creates a clear legal mandate for boards to move beyond checkbox compliance toward a robust accountability matrix.

How often should the board review the organisation’s cyber posture?

Boards should review their organisation's cyber posture at least quarterly, with high-level reporting included in every monthly board pack. The 2023 ASIC Cyber Pulse Survey revealed that 44% of organisations have no incident response plan for supply chain failures. Regular reviews ensure that the board's visibility matches the rapid escalation of the threat landscape. Annual reviews are no longer sufficient to maintain a defensible position against modern ransomware or state-sponsored actors.

What are the top three questions every director should ask their CISO?

Directors must ask their CISO: "What are our crown jewel assets and how are they specifically protected?", "What is our proven recovery time objective for a total system outage?", and "Which third-party vendors hold our most sensitive data?". These questions pivot the conversation from abstract technical data to concrete fiduciary risk. They force a shift from "are we secure?" to "how resilient is our business model under sustained attack?".

Do we need a cyber expert on the board to ensure effective oversight?

You don't necessarily need a technical cyber expert on the board, but every director must possess a baseline level of cyber literacy. Effective oversight requires the ability to challenge management's assumptions rather than just accepting technical reports. According to the Australian Institute of Company Directors (AICD), the focus should be on integrating cyber risk into the existing risk management framework. Independent reviews can bridge the gap between technical complexity and board-level decision-making.

How does AI governance differ from traditional cyber governance?

AI governance expands traditional cyber governance by addressing algorithmic bias, data privacy in training sets, and the unpredictability of generative outputs. While traditional governance focuses on data confidentiality and integrity, AI governance requires oversight of model transparency and ethical use cases. The Australian Government's 2024 Voluntary AI Safety Standard highlights that boards must now manage the specific risks of automated decision-making and the potential for "hallucinations" in corporate data.

Why is an independent review better than an internal audit for cyber?

An independent review provides an unbiased assessment that internal audits often miss due to institutional blind spots or internal politics. Internal teams often mark their own homework, which can lead to a false sense of security for the board. An external, conflict-free advisor exposes uncomfortable truths about your actual readiness. This independence is crucial for building a defensible oversight record that holds up under regulatory scrutiny following a major breach.

What happens during a board-level incident simulation?

A board-level incident simulation pressure-tests your strategic decision-making and communication protocols during a simulated crisis. It isn't a technical drill for IT; it's a high-stakes exercise in crisis leadership and fiduciary responsibility. You'll navigate scenarios like ransom demands, regulatory notifications, and media fallout.
