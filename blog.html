Cyber Governance 8 min read 29 April 2026

The problem with technical comfort

In many organisations, cyber reporting still centres on patch rates, vulnerability counts, uptime, and the status of technical controls. Those metrics have value, but they are not the same as governance. A board that receives green dashboards without a clear narrative about risk ownership, escalation, and residual exposure may be looking at comfort rather than control.

For Australian directors, that gap matters. Regulators, auditors, and stakeholders do not ask whether the endpoint tool was installed. They ask whether the board understood the risk, challenged management, and maintained a defensible oversight process.

What boards should be asking instead

  • check_circleWho owns each material cyber risk at executive and board level?
  • check_circleWhat evidence supports the stated control effectiveness?
  • check_circleHow quickly would the board be informed if the control failed?
  • check_circleCan the organisation demonstrate a pattern of challenge and follow-up in board papers?

Translate metrics into governance

A useful board paper does not stop at numbers. It interprets the numbers in governance terms: what changed, why it matters, what remains unresolved, and what decision the board needs to make. That means linking technical reporting to risk appetite, accountability, incident readiness, and strategic priorities.

The practical shift is simple but powerful. Instead of asking whether a control is deployed, ask whether the board can defend the oversight model behind that control. That is the difference between technical assurance and defensible governance.

A concise board test

Evidence

What proof do we have beyond a dashboard?

Challenge

Where did the board push back or seek clarification?

Escalation

How would this be escalated if the control failed tomorrow?
